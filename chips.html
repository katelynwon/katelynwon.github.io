Building Human Organs From Scratch

Using Microfluidic Chips

Details

  • Date:February 5, 2021

  • Topic:Microfluidics

  • Affiliation:The Knowledge Society

About the Project

Conventional laboratory research requires expensive budgets, tests on animals such as rats or pigs, and can waste chemicals by the barrel. Microfluidic chips, better known as lab-on-a-chips, can be mass-produced, conduct faster experiments, and are multi-functional. I built a tape-based barrier-on chip that models the blood-brain barrier and can be used for drug development for multiple sclerosis. View my video and article on microfluidics and the blood-brain barrier below!

Additional Information

Microfluidic chips can also be used for faster pathogen detection in food, flow cytometry, and even HIV or COVID-19 diagnosis. Some complex microfluidic chips are compartmentalized to model parts of human organs. The ideal microfluidic chip would be one that models an entire organ system, but this has yet to be developed. Despite my lack of access to laboratory equipment, I was able to build my own chip using membrane filters, medical adhesive tape, plastic connectors, and polycarbonate film for under $150.
